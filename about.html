From the Editor's Desk

Why a claw, and where it's clawing next

The AI firehose is not a knowledge problem. It is a triage problem. There is no shortage of announcements; there is a shortage of afternoons. The Claw exists to close that gap — to read everything so the number of things you have to read stays in single digits.

It ranks agent-development news first — multiagent, subagents, skills, tool use, memory, planning — because that is the beat that changes what you build this quarter. Everything else in AI follows. Hard signals (stars and their velocity, points, upvotes, paper attention) build the shortlist; an LLM editor makes the final call and writes, in one plain sentence, why each item matters.

It has a memory. It remembers what it told you yesterday and refuses to sell you the same repository twice — unless the numbers genuinely jumped, in which case it will admit as much.

Where it's headed: clustering the same story across sources into one entry instead of five; a sense of momentum, so you can see what's heating up rather than merely what's loud today; and a daily “if you have an hour, do this” assignment. What it will never become is a feed you have to scroll. That is the entire point of the exercise.

Circulation Department

Who reads The Claw?

One person, mostly: the engineer building agentic systems who has exactly five minutes and strong opinions about how they're spent.

Not researchers chasing citations. Not executives who want a vibe. The reader who needs to know whether anything today is worth acting on — a tool to try, a model to swap in, a pattern worth stealing — before closing the tab and getting back to the actual work.

The decision it speeds up

“Is there anything today I need to act on — or can I get back to work?”

Most days, the honest answer is “get back to work.” The Claw is happy to be the thing that tells you so, in under five minutes, with receipts.

Verdict: usually close the tab

Corrections & Clarifications

What The Claw deliberately does not do

  • It does not predict the future. It reports what shipped, not what a comment thread suspects might ship. Speculation is free and available elsewhere.
  • It does not read X/Twitter. The API is paid and gated, the scraping is fragile and ToS-risky, and we value our remaining will to live. The newsletters already digest the good bits.
  • It does not rank by vibes. Numbers build the shortlist; an editor with reasons makes the cut. If it can't say why something matters, it doesn't run.
  • It does not nag. It publishes once, at 8am Singapore time, and then goes quiet. No push notifications. No “you're all caught up!” No streaks.
  • It does not hand you fifty items. A busy day is eight. If it ever gives you fifty, assume it has been compromised and check the wiring.
